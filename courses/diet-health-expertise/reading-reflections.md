# Reading Reflections
You have a short reading reflection due most days. The syllabus gives you the particular questions you should respond to in your posts.

These are meant to be creative exercises to get you to think holistically about what the reading is trying to do, how it's doing it, how well it succeeds in its aims, and what you thought was most interesting about it (and we all find different things interesting!).

Read the intro, conclusion, skim various bits carefully, and focus on parts that seem more significant or interesting to you.

Get a sense of the tone, style, rhetoric. What's the main argument? What's the evidence to support it? Are you convinced?

Do the necessary work to have an INFORMED opinion. Somewhere in your post, point to a specific passage, example, or moment in the reading that stuck with you — this keeps you anchored in what's actually on the page, not just a general impression of the topic.


## Basics
- ~250 words is the target, but quality over quantity
- Respond to the prompts on the syllabus for each post
- More thorough reflections can earn you extra credit
- Please use your own brain to draft your responses. It's OK to use AI to polish grammar or wording or ask for other ideas, but the reactions and ideas need to be yours. The point of this exercise is the thinking you do while writing it — if AI drafts your reaction for you, you skip the part of the assignment that can actually help you.

## Connect, don't summarize
I (and your classmates) are much more interested in reading about YOUR THINKING AND REACTION, rather than a summary of the assignment.

This exercise is NOT about regurgitating information or identifying the "right" answer. **There is no right answer!** Everyone is going to have a different take on what's interesting, how things connect, how they don't, etc. We can all learn a lot from each other, which is why we post work to the discussion board instead of instructor-only spaces.


## Grading and how these factor in your grade
The idea here is that the reflections should _in sum_ account for a significant portion of your final grade without each one being unduly stressful. So they are low-stakes, yet they should hold everyone accountable for engaging with the reading.


- A: Your post clearly demonstrates broad familiarity with the reading assignment, original thinking, and is clearly articulated.

- B: A solid effort to do the above, but significant shortcoming in several areas.

- C: A decent effort, but doesn't show much engagement with the reading, or is primarily summary rather than original thinking, or doesn't really relate to the prompt.

- D: You posted _something_, but it looks like you were posting without actually engaging with the reading. Often such posts ask questions that are clearly addressed in the readings or are so generic that they could apply to almost any reading on the topic, with no specific reference to what's actually on the page. Also, if your work sounds like AI, you get a D and a chance to redo it.
