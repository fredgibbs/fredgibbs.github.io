## WEEK 1: What Is Thinking? What Is Expertise?

### Session 1: Orientation — "What Do You Already Know (and How)?"

* Ethan Mollick, *Co-Intelligence*, Introduction ("Three Sleepless Nights") and Chapter 1 (~30 pp.)
* Michael I. Jordan, ["Artificial Intelligence—The Revolution Hasn't Happened Yet"](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/wot7mkc1), *Harvard Data Science Review* 1, no. 1 (2019) (~10 pp.) — A computer scientist's argument that what we call "AI" is mostly statistics and that the real intellectual challenges are about human-machine systems, not autonomous intelligence.

#### What does "thinking" mean?

Brief discussion (20 min): When you use Google, are you thinking? When you use spell-check? Where does your cognition end and the tool begin?

#### Activity 1 — AI Baseline Audit (40 min)

Document your current AI use (be honest: homework, email drafting, search, image generation, etc.) in a structured reflection. Then, in pairs, attempt the same task twice: once without AI, once with AI. Suggested task: summarize in 200 words what happened at the Dartmouth Conference of 1956 and why it matters.

* Compare: What did AI add? What did it get wrong? What did you notice about your own process?

#### Activity 2 — The Jagged Frontier Exercise (40 min)

Drawing on Mollick's BCG study concept, test AI on a series of tasks ranging from factual recall to interpretive judgment to creative framing. Rate output quality and document where AI falls inside vs. outside the frontier.

#### Reflection prompt (written, 10 min)

Where on the "jagged frontier" does your own expertise begin?

---

### Session 2: Expertise, Tacit Knowledge, and Machines

* Harry Collins and Robert Evans, *Rethinking Expertise*, Chapters 1–2 (~40 pp.) — Focus on the "Periodic Table of Expertises" and the distinction between "interactional" and "contributory" expertise.
* Hubert Dreyfus, *What Computers Can't Do* (MIT Press, 1972; rev. 1992), pp. 1–25 of the Introduction to the revised edition. Dreyfus's early critique of AI—that embodied experience is irreducible to rules—resonates powerfully with current debates.

#### Brief discussion (20 min)

Collins and Evans argue expertise requires socialization into a community of practice. LLMs have "read" the community's texts but never participated. Is that interactional expertise? Something new? Something fake?

#### Activity — Map Your Expertise (50 min)

Using Collins and Evans's Periodic Table, map your own expertise in a domain you know well, then ask AI to perform tasks at each level (beer-mat knowledge, interactional, contributory). Where does AI convincingly perform? Where does it fail? Document in a shared Google Doc.

#### Activity — Prompt as Question Formation (30 min)

Introduction to deliberate prompting. Practice writing prompts for AI as a form of question design: vague prompts → specific prompts → prompts that specify persona, audience, constraints. 

**Emphasis:** The quality of the question determines the quality of the answer. This is not a technical skill; it is the oldest intellectual skill there is.

## WEEK 2: A Short History of Artificial Intelligence—and What "Intelligence" Means

### Session 3: From Turing to Transformers

* Melanie Mitchell, *Artificial Intelligence: A Guide for Thinking Humans*, Chapters 1–3 (~45 pp.) — From the Dartmouth conference through expert systems to neural networks.
* Alan Turing, ["Computing Machinery and Intelligence"](http://www.loebner.net/Prizef/TuringArticle.html), *Mind* 59, no. 236 (1950): 433–460. Read at least Sections 1–4 (~12 pp.). The original framing of "can machines think?" and the Imitation Game.

#### Brief discussion (15 min)

Why did the founders of AI think intelligence could be formalized? What assumptions about the mind does that require?

#### Activity — Turing Test, Updated (50 min)

Design and administer your own Turing-style tests. In groups of 3, one person writes responses to questions, one person gets AI responses to the same questions, and one person (blind to source) tries to distinguish them. Vary the domain: factual history, emotional narrative, ethical reasoning, creative writing.

* Debrief: What kinds of "thinking" are easiest to fake? Hardest? What does that tell us?

#### Activity — Timeline of Intelligence Definitions (40 min)

Using AI as a research assistant, build a collaborative timeline of how "intelligence" has been defined across disciplines: philosophy (Aristotle's *nous*, Descartes's *cogito*), psychology (Binet's IQ tests, Gardner's multiple intelligences), computer science (Turing, Minsky, McCarthy), cognitive science (embodied cognition, extended mind thesis). 

Product: a shared document that reveals how definitions of intelligence always reflect the anxieties and tools of their era.



### Session 4: What Does "Knowing" Mean When Machines Produce Knowledge?

* Emily Bender et al., ["On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?"](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922) FAccT '21 (2021): 610–623. The "stochastic parrots" paper argues LLMs produce text without understanding—a direct challenge to claims of AI knowledge.
* Steven Shapin, *A Social History of Truth: Civility and Science in Seventeenth-Century England* (University of Chicago Press, 1994), Chapter 1 ("The Great Civility"), pp. 3–41. Shapin shows that even scientific knowledge depends on trust, social standing, and credibility—frameworks now disrupted by AI.

#### Brief discussion (20 min)

Shapin argues truth has always been social. If we start trusting AI outputs as "knowledge," what social contract is being rewritten?

#### Activity — AI as Witness (60 min)

Drawing on Shapin's framework of gentlemanly credibility, "cross-examine" AI on a historical topic:

* Ask AI to explain a historical event (e.g., the Flexner Report's impact on medical education)
* Probe for sources, ask it to distinguish established fact from interpretation, request counterarguments
* Evaluate: Would you cite this "testimony"? Under what conditions?

#### Mini-lecture (15 min)

Brief overview of how LLMs actually work—tokenization, attention, prediction—just enough to understand that AI generates probabilistically, not through comprehension. Use Mollick's analogy of the "alien mind."

## WEEK 3: Finding and Evaluating Sources—With and Against AI

### Session 5: Source Discovery and the Problem of Trust

* Sam Wineburg, *Why Learn History*, Chapter 1 ("Why Google Can't Save Us") (~20 pp.)
* Sam Wineburg and Sarah McGrew, ["Lateral Reading and the Nature of Expertise: Reading Less and Learning More When Evaluating Digital Information"](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2953675), *Teachers College Record* 121 (2019) (~30 pp.). The landmark study showing fact-checkers evaluate sources by leaving the page (lateral reading), while historians and students stay on the page (vertical reading).

#### Brief discussion (15 min)

What changes when AI can generate plausible-looking sources? How does lateral reading apply when the "source" is an AI output?

#### Activity — Source Discovery Race (60 min)

Students receive a historical research question (e.g., "What role did women play in New Mexico's public health campaigns during the 1918 flu pandemic?"). Three rounds:

1. Traditional search only (library databases, Google Scholar)
2. AI-assisted search (asking AI to suggest sources, archives, search terms)
3. AI + verification (using AI suggestions but fact-checking every claim)

Compare: Which round produced the best sources? Which produced phantom citations? How many AI-suggested sources actually exist?

#### Activity — Lateral Reading with AI (30 min)

Practice Wineburg's lateral reading technique on AI outputs: when AI makes a claim, leave the conversation, verify independently, then return. Develop a personal protocol.

#### Introduce NotebookLM (15 min)

Demonstration of Google NotebookLM as a source-grounded research tool. Upload 2–3 primary sources on a topic and show how NotebookLM's responses are anchored to those specific documents—contrasting with the "anything goes" nature of general chatbots.

### Session 6: Trustworthiness, Bias, and the History of Information Authority

* Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression*, Chapter 1 ("A Society, Searching") (~30 pp.)
* Adrian Johns, *The Nature of the Book: Print and Knowledge in the Making* (University of Chicago Press, 1998), Introduction (~20 pp.). Johns demonstrates that print didn't automatically create reliable knowledge—trust in printed texts had to be constructed through social and institutional practices. A powerful historical parallel to our current moment with AI.

#### Brief discussion (15 min)

Noble shows bias is built into search. Johns shows bias was built into print. What patterns recur?

#### Activity — Bias Audit (60 min)

Pick a historical topic with known interpretive disputes (e.g., the causes of the Mexican-American War, or the legacy of the Dawes Act) and ask multiple AI models the same question. Compare outputs for:

* Whose perspective is centered?
* What sources are cited or implied?
* What is omitted?
* Does the framing change with different prompt phrasing?

Document findings in a structured "bias audit" template.

#### Activity — Introduction to the Course Project (30 min)

Introduce the Xanthan framework and the collaborative website project. Students choose (or are assigned) a historical moment in the history of learning technologies. Begin brainstorming with AI assistance.

## WEEK 4: Synthesis, Argument, and AI as Thinking Partner

### Session 7: Summarizing and Synthesizing—What AI Does Well and What It Flattens

* Ethan Mollick, *Co-Intelligence*, Chapter 5 ("AI as Creative") (~20 pp.)
* [Instructor: Choose a history-of-science article from your own area of expertise with a strong argument that requires interpretive judgment to summarize well]

#### Activity — The Summarization Challenge (60 min)

Read the assigned article carefully (in class, with annotation). Then:

1. Write a 150-word summary of the article's argument (not just its topic)
2. Ask AI to summarize the same article (provide the text via NotebookLM or paste)
3. Compare: Does AI capture the argument or just the topic? Does it preserve nuance? Does it flatten interpretive claims into neutral descriptions?
4. Revise your own summary using AI feedback, then revise the AI summary using your own judgment.

**Debrief:** What does "synthesis" require that "summarization" doesn't?

#### Activity — Constructing an Argument with AI (40 min)

Practice using AI as a sounding board for historical argument:

* State a thesis about your project topic
* Ask AI to steelman the thesis, then to challenge it
* Ask AI for counterevidence, alternative interpretations, and potential objections
* Revise the thesis based on the exchange

**Emphasis:** AI is most useful not when it gives you answers, but when it gives you better questions.

#### Book Review Assignment Introduced

You will write an AI-assisted book review (see Week 5 for details).

### Session 8: Perspectives, Omissions, and Groundtruthing

* Dipesh Chakrabarty, *Provincializing Europe: Postcolonial Thought and Historical Difference* (Princeton University Press, 2000), Introduction (~25 pp.). On whose knowledge counts and whose frameworks dominate.


#### Activity — The Missing Voices Exercise (50 min)

Take a standard historical narrative (e.g., the building of the transcontinental railroad, or the Manhattan Project in New Mexico) and systematically ask AI:

* "What perspectives are missing from this account?"
* "Rewrite this from the perspective of [specific marginalized group]"
* "What sources would I need to find to tell a fuller story?"
* "What do we NOT know about this topic, and why?"

Then: evaluate AI's responses. Are the "missing perspectives" it generates substantive or tokenistic? Does it cite real sources? How do you distinguish genuine historiographic insight from AI-generated platitudes about "diverse perspectives"?

#### Activity — Groundtruthing Protocol (40 min)

Develop and test a personal "groundtruthing" protocol for AI-assisted research:

1. What claims need independent verification?
2. What verification methods are appropriate for different claim types?
3. When is an AI output good enough to use, and when does it require human judgment?
4. How do you document AI contributions in your work?

**Produce:** A one-page "Groundtruthing Checklist" that you will refine throughout the course.

## WEEK 5: The Book Review and Designing Learning

### Session 9: Writing a Book Review with AI — Process, Not Product

#### Pre-reading

* Naomi Oreskes and Erik M. Conway, *Merchants of Doubt: How a Handful of Scientists Obscured the Truth on Issues from Tobacco Smoke to Global Warming* (Bloomsbury, 2010), Chapters 1–2 and Conclusion (~60 pp.). Thematically rich for the course: expertise, trust, manufactured uncertainty.

#### Activity — The AI-Assisted Book Review (Full session, ~100 min)

This is a signature assignment. You will write a book review of the assigned text, using AI as a deliberate partner at each stage, while documenting your process:

**Stage 1 (20 min):** Generate initial impressions without AI. What is the book arguing? What evidence is strongest? What questions remain?

**Stage 2 (25 min):** Ask AI to summarize the book, identify its key contributions, and place it in scholarly context. Compare AI's reading with your own. Where does AI's summary diverge from your interpretation?

**Stage 3 (25 min):** Draft a review. Use AI to help with structure, transitions, or phrasing, but maintain your interpretive argument. Ask AI: "What am I missing?" "What would a critic say?"

**Stage 4 (15 min):** Use AI to identify weaknesses in your review. Ask it to play the role of a skeptical reviewer. Revise.

**Stage 5 (15 min):** Write a 200-word "Process Reflection" documenting exactly how AI shaped the review and where you overrode its suggestions.

#### Deliverable

Book review (800–1000 words) + Process Reflection (200 words). Graded on the quality of the review AND the sophistication of the reflection.

### Session 10: Designing an Assignment — Students as Pedagogues

* Ethan Mollick and Lilach Mollick, ["Assigning AI: Seven Approaches for Students, with Prompts"](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4475995) (Wharton, 2023) (~15 pp.)
* Ethan Mollick, *Co-Intelligence*, Chapter 7 ("AI as Teacher") (~15 pp.)


#### Activity — AI-assist some other course
"Convert" an exercise from another class. How could AI help you LEARN MORE?

* Use AI in a specific, bounded way
* Have a clear learning objective
* Include a metacognitive reflection component
* Be testable in class today

**Phase 2 — Test (30 min):** Groups swap assignments and complete each other's. Document what works, what's confusing, where AI helps, where it distracts.

**Phase 3 — Debrief (20 min):** Each group reports on the assignment they tested. Class discussion: What makes an AI-integrated assignment good vs. gimmicky? How do you prevent the AI from doing the thinking FOR the student?

**Key principle to surface:** Good AI assignments make the student's thinking MORE visible, not less. If AI can complete the assignment without the student learning anything, the assignment has failed.

### Session 11: Working with Xanthan and AI — Building Your Contribution

* Xanthan documentation: "Getting Started," "Using AI Assistance," and "ScrollStories Overview" at https://xanthan-web.github.io
* Bethany Nowviskie, "Capacity through Care," in *Debates in the Digital Humanities 2019*, ed. Matthew K. Gold and Lauren F. Klein (University of Minnesota Press, 2019). On sustainability, care, and collaboration in digital humanities work.

#### Workshop Session — Hands-On Building (Full session)

This is a working session. You will:

3. **Build the page (30 min):** Using Xanthan's templates and AI assistance for formatting/troubleshooting, create your page.

4. **Peer review (20 min):** Pair up and review each other's pages for historical accuracy, argument quality, and AI documentation.

### Session 12: Project Workshop and the Ethics of AI-Assisted Scholarship

* Kate Crawford, *The Atlas of AI: Power, Politics, and the Planetary Costs of Artificial Intelligence* (Yale University Press, 2021), Introduction (~20 pp.). On the material costs—labor, resources, environmental—behind AI systems.
* Your institution's current AI policy (if one exists) + 2–3 other universities' policies for comparison.

#### Workshop (60 min)

Continue building Xanthan pages. Instructor provides 1:1 consultations.

#### Discussion — Ethics of AI-Assisted Scholarship (45 min)

Now that you have spent 5 weeks using AI for research and writing, you have experiential grounding for an ethics conversation:

* When should AI use be disclosed? Always? Sometimes? Never?
* Does AI-assisted work count as "your" work?
* What are the environmental and labor costs of the AI tools you've been using?
* How should academic integrity policies change?
* What would you recommend to [your institution]?

#### Activity (15 min)

Draft a "Personal AI Ethics Statement" (200 words) articulating your own principles for AI use in academic work.

## WEEK 7: Reflection, Presentation, and What Comes Next

### Session 13: Project Presentations and Peer Feedback

#### Activity — Lightning Presentations (90 min)

Each student presents their Xanthan contribution (5 min each):

* What historical moment did you research?
* How did AI help? Where did it mislead?
* What's one thing you know now that you didn't know in Week 1—about history, about AI, or about your own thinking?

Peer feedback using a structured rubric that evaluates both historical quality and AI-critical reflection.

#### Activity — Collective Site Review (30 min)

View the assembled website together. Discuss: What story does the collective site tell about the history of learning? What's missing? What would you add with more time?

### Session 14: Metacognitive Wrap-Up — What Has Changed?

#### Pre-reading

Re-read your own Week 1 "AI Baseline Audit." Come prepared to discuss what has shifted.

#### Activity — Then and Now Reflection (30 min)

Revisit your Week 1 baseline audit and write a comparative reflection:

* How has your AI use changed?
* What skills have you developed?
* What are you still uncertain about?
* How would you explain "thinking with AI" to someone who hasn't taken this course?

#### Discussion — The Futures Question (40 min)

Return to the course's driving question: How can AI help us think more critically, develop skills, and produce higher-quality work?

Connect to workforce implications: What AI skills will students need in 5 years? What human skills become MORE important, not less?

#### Activity — Letter to Future Students (20 min)

Each student writes a letter to the next cohort: What do you wish you'd known? What advice would you give? What's the one thing you'd tell them not to skip?

Reminder: Final reflective summary (1–2 pages) due one week after last class.
